Abstract
SAFORA is a ride-hailing mobile application built for the Pakistani market, where passenger safety, particularly for women, has long been a concern that existing platforms have not seriously addressed. The idea came from watching how Careem and InDriver work in cities like Lahore and Sialkot. Both are useful but neither has a real mechanism to stop a driver from going off-route or to confirm that the person behind the wheel is actually who they registered as.
Honestly Phase 2 forced us to make calls we had been putting off. Where does the AI service actually sit? Does SafetySentinel run on the device or on the server? Those were not resolved in Phase 1 and sorting them out took longer than expected. What you are reading is where we landed. Most of the design effort went into Pink Pass, SafetySentinel, and the pricing logic, and that shows in how much of the document covers those three things.
On the tech side, the backend runs on Node.js. The passenger and driver apps are built with React Native and Expo, which means they run as a PWA in the browser and can also be packaged for iOS and Android. The pricing model sits in a separate Python process and we kept it that way mainly because running scikit-learn inside a Node server would have made deployment much more complicated than it needed to be. MongoDB Atlas handles data storage and Socket.io keeps the live connection open between the app and the server during trips.
Introduction
Product
SAFORA has two sides: a passenger app and a driver app. On the surface it does what any ride-hailing app does, you book, you track, you pay. But the decisions we made during design kept coming back to one question: what happens when something goes wrong during a trip? That question is what pushed us toward biometric driver verification, which makes it much harder for someone to drive under a fake profile, and the Pink Pass category, which we restricted entirely to verified female drivers. SafetySentinel came from the same thinking. We wanted something that did not require the passenger to do anything actively. It just watches. If the route goes wrong and the passenger misses the check-in, the SOS goes out on its own.
Background
Careem showed up in Pakistan around 2016 and Uber followed shortly after, then InDriver came in with a different pricing model. Lahore took to it quickly. Cheaper than a taxi, easier than calling someone. But the safety complaints started showing up alongside the growth and they never really stopped. Drivers going off-route, passengers not being sure the driver matched the profile, harassment that got reported but not really prevented. The platforms had rating systems but a rating after the fact does not help someone who is already in a bad situation.
That gap is what SAFORA is trying to address. Pink Pass restricts a whole ride category to verified female drivers only, no exceptions. SafetySentinel watches the GPS during every trip and if the route deviation goes beyond what is acceptable, the passenger gets a prompt. No response in 30 seconds and the SOS fires on its own. The pricing engine uses a trained Linear Regression model so fares are not just a flat formula, they adjust based on demand and time. And the Emergency SOS button is always one tap away, sending live location to whoever the passenger has listed as an emergency contact
Objective
The main things we are trying to deliver with this project are listed here.
Getting a working cross-platform app onto iOS and Android using React Native is the base requirement. Beyond that, the pricing model needs to stay within 8 percent error on fare prediction, otherwise the estimates passengers see before booking will not be trustworthy. SafetySentinel has to respond fast enough to matter, which we set at 30 seconds from deviation detection to SOS trigger. Pink Pass requires a full verification pipeline covering biometric enrollment and CNIC confirmation before a driver can be approved for that category. Two seconds is our threshold for location update latency. Past that, the live map starts drifting and becomes more confusing than helpful. The Urdu requirement came directly from talking to people in Sialkot. A lot of them switch apps to Urdu by default and if ours does not support that, we lose them immediately.
Scope
SAFORA has four parts. The rider-facing side is the Passenger App. It handles booking, shows the driver live during the trip, lets the passenger trigger SOS, and collects payment at the end. Drivers work from a completely separate app because what they need is nothing like what a passenger needs. Trip requests arrive there, navigation runs from it, and it is where drivers check what they earned. The admin side is not really an app in the traditional sense. Think of it as an internal operations tool that the SAFORA team uses to approve or reject driver applications, keep an eye on trips that are still running, and respond to incident reports. Sitting behind all of that is the AI microservice, a small Python server with one job: fare prediction. It does not touch user records at all.
We drew a clear line on what this project does not include. No physical payment hardware, no vehicle ownership management, no delivery or logistics functionality. Those would be separate products and are not part of this phase
Business Goals
Lahore is where we are launching first. It is the biggest ride-hailing market in Pakistan and if the safety angle lands anywhere, it will land there. Five percent of local ride-hailing volume in year one is the number we set as a first milestone, and honestly that is already ambitious for a new platform with no existing user base.
Female user retention is one of the metrics we care about most. The Pink Pass guarantee only works if women actually keep using it, so 80 percent retention is the benchmark we are working toward. On safety incidents we are targeting a 60 percent reduction compared to what riders on existing platforms currently report, which is admittedly ambitious but that is the whole point of the product.
Revenue comes from two places. A 15 percent commission on every completed ride, and a monthly subscription that drivers pay to be eligible for Pink Pass bookings. The subscription creates a trust signal since drivers who pay for that tier have more to lose if their verification lapses.
Document Conventions
All architectural and flow diagrams presented in this document have been drawn manually to ensure the highest level of technical precision and detail. No automated software or generative design tools were used for the creation of these visuals. The notation follows the UML 2.5 standard throughout. The SAFORA visual brand identity is maintained through a specific color palette: Yellow (#F5B800) is used as the primary action color, White (#FFFFFF) for backgrounds, and Black (#000000) for primary text, ensuring consistency with the live mobile and web interfaces. Also worth mentioning: whenever this document uses the words Passenger, Driver, or Admin, those are system roles. They do not refer to any real specific person.
Miscellaneous
Pakistan's Personal Data Protection Bill 2023 came up early in the design process when we were deciding how to handle biometric verification. The rule we set for ourselves was simple: raw biometric data never leaves the device. What happens on the device is that a hash gets generated using the built-in secure storage, Android KeyStore on Android and Secure Enclave on iPhones. Only that hash reaches our server. We made this call before writing a single line of backend code so the architecture was never built to receive raw biometric data in the first place.
Technical Architecture
SAFORA is structured as a modular monolith with one service intentionally separated out. The Node.js and Express server handles everything except fare prediction: authentication, ride lifecycle management, driver matching, SafetySentinel GPS monitoring, SOS alert creation, and in-app chat. The only reason the AI component is a separate Python Flask process is that scikit-learn runs in Python and embedding a Python runtime inside a Node.js server creates dependency management problems that compound over time.
Application and Data Architecture
The application is divided into three logical layers.
The Presentation Layer consists of the Passenger App at safora.me, the Driver App also at safora.me under the driver routing path, and the Admin Dashboard at admin.safora.me. Both passenger and driver apps are React Native with Expo compiled to Progressive Web Apps deployed on Vercel. The Passenger and Driver apps share the same codebase and use role-based navigation to show the correct screen stack after login.
The Service Orchestration Layer is the Node.js Express server running on Railway at Port 5000. It handles JWT authentication, all ride lifecycle state transitions enforced through the RideStateMachine utility, driver geospatial matching using MongoDB's $near operator, SafetySentinel monitoring, SOS alert creation and Twilio SMS dispatch, Pink Pass enrollment, and admin operations. Socket.io runs within the same Node.js process on the same server using the http.createServer wrapper rather than a separate port ΓÇö the Socket.io server shares the HTTP server instance.
The Analytical Layer is the Python Flask service running on Railway. It receives fare prediction requests at POST /api/pricing/predict and returns an estimated fare in PKR using a
trained scikit-learn LinearRegression model. It also handles Pink Pass liveness frame verification at POST /api/pink-pass/verify-frames using OpenCV Haar Cascade detection.

Component Interactions and Collaborations
The interaction flow between components follows this pattern for the most critical path, which is a passenger booking a ride:
The Passenger App sends POST /api/rides/request to the Node.js backend with pickup coordinates, dropoff coordinates, and ride type. The backend calculates distance using the Haversine formula, then makes an internal HTTP call to the AI service at POST
/api/pricing/predict with a 3000ms timeout. If the AI service is unavailable, the fallback formula (distance ├ù 35) + (duration ├ù 5) + 50 is used. A Ride document is created in MongoDB with status requested. The backend then queries the Driver collection using $near with
$maxDistance: 15000 metres. For Pink Pass rides, it filters further for drivers where user.gender === 'female' and pinkPassCertified === true. When a match is found, the backend emits a ride:request Socket.io event to the room driver:<driverDocId>. The driver app receives this event and shows the request with a countdown timer.

Design Reuse and Design Patterns
Six design patterns are applied across the SAFORA codebase. MVC is applied in the Node.js backend through the separation of route handlers as controllers, Mongoose models as the data layer, and JSON response formatting as the view layer. The Repository Pattern abstracts all MongoDB queries behind Mongoose model methods so that the controller layer never writes raw query objects. The Observer Pattern is used in the Socket.io event system where both the passenger app and driver app subscribe to ride events, and SafetySentinel acts as an observer that publishes safety alerts when GPS data arrives. The Strategy Pattern encapsulates the LinearRegression algorithm in the AI microservice so the model can be retrained and swapped without changing the prediction endpoint contract. The Singleton Pattern ensures a single MongoDB connection pool and a single Redis client instance across the entire application lifecycle. The Factory Pattern is applied in the notification service which creates SMS via Twilio, push via Firebase FCM, or Socket.io in-app messages depending on the notification type and app state.
Additionally, the State Pattern is implemented in the RideStateMachine utility class which explicitly defines the valid state transitions for the ride lifecycle: requested ΓåÆ matched ΓåÆ accepted ΓåÆ started ΓåÆ completed, with cancelled reachable from any state before completed. Invalid transitions throw an error before any database write occurs.
Technology Architecture
The backend API and AI microservice are both deployed on Railway. The Passenger PWA and Admin Dashboard are deployed on Vercel. MongoDB Atlas M10 is used as the primary database ΓÇö the M10 tier specifically because the free tier does not support the $near geospatial operator. Redis runs as a Railway addon with a 30-second TTL on driver location keys. Firebase provides phone authentication for OTP delivery to Pakistani mobile numbers. Twilio handles SOS emergency contact SMS. Google Maps Platform handles location autocomplete, geocoding, and map rendering in the passenger app.

Architecture Evaluation
The following explains why each major technology was chosen over its alternatives, based on actual constraints encountered during development.
Node.js instead of Django or Laravel for the backend: The Socket.io library is native to the Node.js ecosystem. Running real-time WebSocket communication through a Python or PHP server would require a bridge process, which adds a failure point and latency. The Node.js event loop handles thousands of concurrent WebSocket connections on a single thread without the overhead of spawning a thread per connection as a threaded framework would. The ESM module syntax used throughout the codebase requires Node.js 20 LTS as a hard minimum.
React Native with Expo instead of Flutter: JavaScript is shared between the frontend and the backend, which matters significantly for a two-member team where both members need to read each other's code. The Expo PWA build target specifically allowed deployment to safora.me as a browser-accessible application without requiring App Store approval, which was essential for the testing timeline. Flutter would have required a separate Dart codebase with no code sharing with the backend.
Python Flask for the AI service instead of a Node.js ML library: The scikit-learn LinearRegression model was trained and serialized in Python. The serialized model file is Python-version-specific ΓÇö an attempt to load a model trained on Python 3.11 in Python 3.9 fails at deserialization. Running a separate Flask process means the model can be retrained independently and the process can be restarted without touching the main backend. Node.js ML libraries like Brain.js were evaluated and rejected because they do not produce comparable accuracy for tabular regression problems.
MongoDB instead of PostgreSQL: Ride documents have variable structures. A standard ride document has different fields from a Pink Pass ride, and the SOS alert document contains nested GeoJSON coordinates. MongoDB's document model fits this naturally without nullable columns. The $near operator with 2dsphere indexing on the Driver collection is essential for
the driver matching query ΓÇö this geospatial support is built into MongoDB and would require PostGIS as an extension in PostgreSQL, adding deployment complexity.
Turf.js for SafetySentinel instead of the Google Maps Distance Matrix API: SafetySentinel calls safetySentinel.updateLocation(rideId, coordinates) every time a driver GPS ping arrives over Socket.io. During an active trip this occurs roughly every 5 seconds. At scale, using the Google Maps Distance Matrix API for this would generate thousands of paid API calls per minute. Turf.js runs entirely within the Node.js process using the @turf/turf npm package at version 6.5.0, with zero external network calls, zero per-call cost, and sub-millisecond execution time per calculation.
Railway instead of AWS EC2 for hosting: Railway deploys directly from a GitHub repository on every push to the main branch, injects environment variables from the project settings, and manages SSL certificates automatically. For a two-member student team without dedicated DevOps experience, this reduces deployment from a multi-hour manual process on EC2 to a sub-five-minute automated deployment. The trade-off is less control over hardware configuration, which is acceptable at the current scale.
Detailed/Component Design
Component-Component Interface
The four internal component boundaries in SAFORA each have defined communication contracts.
Passenger App to Node.js Backend: All non-real-time requests use HTTPS REST over Port 5000. Every request except login includes an Authorization: Bearer <token> header containing the SAFORA JWT issued after Firebase Phone Auth verification. The JWT payload contains { userId, role } and is signed with HS256. The access token expires in 15 minutes. The passenger app stores the token in AsyncStorage and includes it on every API call.
Driver App to Node.js Backend: The driver app authenticates through the same Firebase Phone Auth flow as passengers. After receiving the SAFORA JWT, the driver app calls GET
/api/drivers/me to retrieve the Driver document. This is critical because the Driver document _id is different from the User document _id, and Socket.io ride request events are emitted to the room driver:<driverDocId> using the Driver document _id. The driver app then emits a join:driver Socket.io event with this Driver _id to join the correct room. An earlier bug existed where the User
_id was used instead of the Driver _id, which meant the driver never received ride requests ΓÇö this was resolved by always using the id field from the /api/drivers/me response.
Node.js  Backend  to  Python  AI  Service:  Internal  HTTP  call  using  Axios  to  POST
{AI_SERVICE_URL}/api/pricing/predict. The request body contains { distance, duration, time_of_day, day_of_week, demand_level, origin_area, traffic_multiplier }. The timeout is 3000 milliseconds. If the AI service times out or is unreachable, the backend silently falls back to the formula price and the passenger never sees an error. The AI service URL is stored in the AI_SERVICE_URL environment variable.
Node.js Backend to SafetySentinel: SafetySentinel is instantiated as a singleton at server startup: const safetySentinel = new SafetySentinel(io) and stored on the Express app object using app.set('safetySentinel', safetySentinel). When a ride status changes to started via PATCH
/api/rides/:id/status,  the  backend  calls  safetySentinel.startMonitoring(ride._id.toString(),
plannedRoute) where plannedRoute is a two-coordinate array [pickupCoordinates, dropoffCoordinates]. Every Socket.io driver:location-update event triggers safetySentinel.updateLocation(rideId, { lat, lng }). The deviation threshold is DEVIATION_THRESHOLD = 500 metres and the sustained deviation duration before alert is DEVIATION_DURATION = 30000 milliseconds.
Node.js Backend to Redis: The cacheDriverLocation(driverId, lat, lng) function stores driver coordinates at key driver:loc:<driverId> with a 30-second TTL using setex. Session tokens are cached at session:<userId> with a 900-second TTL. If Redis is unavailable or REDIS_URL is not set, a JavaScript Map-based in-memory fallback is used automatically with the same API surface, so all callers work without modification.

Component-Human Interface
SAFORA has three distinct human-facing interfaces.
Passenger App (safora.me): The screen flow begins at the Welcome screen where the user selects language (English or Urdu) and role. After selecting Passenger the user enters their phone number and Firebase Phone Auth sends the OTP. On verification the SAFORA JWT is issued. If the returned isNewUser flag is true, the user is taken to a Profile Setup screen to enter their name and email. After profile completion the Home Dashboard loads showing a Google Maps view centered
on the user's detected location with nearby driver markers. Tapping "Where to?" opens the Booking screen. After entering pickup and dropoff the fare estimate is displayed. On confirming, the Searching screen shows a matching animation. Once a driver is matched the Tracking screen shows the driver's live position updating every few seconds via Socket.io. During an active trip the SOS button is always visible. After the trip ends the Rating screen collects feedback and shows the payment summary.
Driver App (safora.me ΓÇö driver routing path): After Firebase Phone Auth login the Driver Dashboard loads showing online/offline toggle, today's earnings, total trips, average rating, and a live map of the driver's current location. When the driver is online and a ride request arrives via Socket.io, the Ride Request screen appears with a 30-second countdown, the passenger's pickup and dropoff addresses, estimated fare, and swipe-to-accept control. After accepting, the Trip Navigation screen shows the current trip phase progressing through Arrived, Onboard, Dropping, and Done states. GPS coordinates are broadcast to the backend via Socket.io driver:location-update events while the trip is in Started status.
Admin Dashboard (admin.safora.me): The admin logs in using a phone number where the OTP is rerouted to a master email address. The Dashboard Overview shows total rides today, active driver count, SOS alerts count, and registered passenger count. The Live Map panel shows driver positions in Lahore updated by Socket.io. The Drivers panel shows pending background check approvals with approve and reject buttons. The SOS Alerts panel shows active alerts with passenger name, location, and status. The Passengers panel shows the full user list and a Pink Pass verification queue. The Admins panel allows the master admin to add and remove admin accounts.

Additional Information
Contrast ratio came up early in the design process. The WCAG AA floor is 4.5 to 1 and the yellow-on-white combination in the SAFORA palette clears that without adjustment. Touch targets ended up at 44 by 44 points. That is an Apple guideline but we were also thinking about the SOS button specifically. In a genuinely scary situation, fine motor control is the last thing you can rely on.
Localization was not something we planned to add at the end. The structure for it went in from the beginning. Text does not live inside components as hardcoded strings. Every piece of copy goes through an i18n key and the actual words come from a translation file swapped out at runtime. Urdu mode flips the whole layout horizontally and React Native's I18nManager takes care of that. We wired it into the language selection screen so it applies immediately when the user picks Urdu.
Other Design Details
SafetySentinel (Technical Design)
SafetySentinel is a server-side background process. Nothing happens until a ride moves to onboard status, which was a deliberate decision. We did not want it active during driver search or at pickup because an alert before the trip actually starts would be confusing and would train passengers to ignore the prompts.
Turf.js does the geometry work. We picked it specifically because it is a JavaScript library and runs inside the same Node.js process, no external service call required. Calling out to Python just for distance calculations would have added latency and one more thing that could fail.
What actually happens during a trip: when the ride starts, the planned Google Maps route gets stored as a GeoJSON LineString in the ride record. Every 5 seconds the Driver App sends a location ping over Socket.io. What SafetySentinel does with each incoming coordinate is look up the closest point on the saved route to where the driver actually is. That distance is the deviation reading. Once it breaks 500 metres the passenger gets a safety prompt on screen. A 30 second timer is running from that point. No response and the alert fires.

AI Pricing Model (Technical Design)
Linear Regression ended up being the right call for fare prediction, though we looked at other options. Ride fares in an urban context follow a roughly linear pattern. Distance goes up, fare goes up. Demand goes up, fare goes up. There is no complicated relationship that would require something heavier. Speed was the other factor. Anyone who has used a ride app knows the fare
screen is where you decide whether to book. A response that takes even half a second longer than expected feels like a loading bug. The model runs in under 100 milliseconds which keeps that screen feeling instant.
The implementation uses scikit-learn's LinearRegression.

Training records come from historical ride datasets with similar trip profiles. Security Design
Security was not something we designed around at the end. A few decisions were made early enough that they shaped the architecture. Biometric data never leaving the device was the first one, because once you start receiving raw biometrics on a server you inherit a whole set of storage and compliance obligations we did not want. The device generates a hash using its own secure hardware and only the hash travels across the wire. JWT authentication uses short-lived 15 minute access tokens so a stolen token has a small window. OTP codes expire in 5 minutes and lock out after 3 failed attempts to prevent brute force. Rate limiting sits at 100 requests per minute per IP. Helmet.js handles the HTTP security header setup automatically. On the database side, Atlas only accepts connections from whitelisted IPs and the stored data is encrypted at rest over TLS 1.3.
Schema validation through Joi runs before anything from a request body reaches actual business logic, which cuts off a whole category of injection risk at the entry point.
